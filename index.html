From the AI Winter
to the ChatGPT Explosion

A computational history of how “artificial intelligence” was written and talked about — in books and in the news — from 1990 to 2023.

When did artificial intelligence become the phrase that saturates our bookshelves and our headlines? The intuitive answer — “recently, with ChatGPT” — turns out to be only the last act of a much longer and stranger story. Tracing two corpora across three decades — the text of millions of books (via Google Books Ngram) and the volume of global news coverage (via Media Cloud) — reveals an arc that runs from disrepute, through a quiet rebranding, to sudden cultural saturation.

Act I  ·  1990–2013

The Winter and the Rebranding

The story does not begin with growth — it begins with decline. Through the 1990s, the phrase “artificial intelligence” steadily falls in books. This is the long shadow of the AI Winter: after 1980s expert systems over-promised and the specialized-hardware market collapsed around 1987, funding evaporated and “AI” carried a stigma of hype.

Researchers responded not by quitting, but by renaming. “Machine learning” — statistical, empirical, and modest in its claims — climbs steadily across the same period and overtakes “artificial intelligence” around 2013. The vocabulary shift is itself a historical argument: a field distancing itself from a discredited label by reframing its work as concrete, data-driven method rather than grand ambition.

Act II  ·  2012–2021

Back into the Light

Two forces rescued “AI.” First, a technical breakthrough: deep neural networks, powered by GPUs and massive datasets, suddenly worked — the 2012 ImageNet moment turned a fringe approach into the dominant one. Second, public spectacle: IBM Watson winning Jeopardy! (2011), and above all AlphaGo's defeat of Lee Sedol (2016), a result experts had thought a decade away.

The press took notice. Across the 2010s, the number of news articles mentioning AI climbs from a few thousand to tens of thousands a year. Artificial intelligence was migrating from the lab bench to the front page — no longer a niche research topic, but a recurring subject of public attention.

Act III  ·  2022–2023

The ChatGPT Explosion

Then came ChatGPT. Released in November 2022, it placed a fluent, general-purpose AI in the hands of anyone with a browser, and the discourse detonated. News volume leapt to the highest point in the entire series — but the more telling measure is share. Normalizing for the overall growth of online news, AI's slice of all coverage more than doubled in a single year, reaching roughly 1% of everything in the press.

That distinction matters: the spike is not merely “more articles being published in general.” It is a genuine shift in collective attention. The label “artificial intelligence,” nearly abandoned thirty years earlier, had become inescapable.

One story, two speeds

The two corpora narrate the same history at different tempos. Books — slow, deliberate, edited over years — recorded a quiet rebranding: a field swapping a tarnished name for a working one. News — fast and reactive — recorded an explosion: a phrase vaulting from professional embarrassment to household word. Read together, they trace how a single term travels from the margins of credibility to the center of public life.
