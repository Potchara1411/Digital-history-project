A computational history of AI discourse, 1990–2023 — treating media coverage of artificial intelligence as a primary source for how societies negotiate power, fear, and technological change.

Framing

Filling a gap in Atlas of AI

Kate Crawford's Atlas of AI argues powerfully that artificial intelligence is a system of power and extraction — a “registry of power.”

It is a compelling qualitative argument. But it never proves computationally when public discourse around AI actually shifted, or how fast that shift happened. — the gap this project sets out to fill

This project supplies that missing evidence: thirty years of book data (Google Ngram) and thirteen years of global news data (Media Cloud, GDELT), used to locate the turning points and measure their speed. In doing so it flips the usual digital-humanities question. Instead of asking what AI can do for historians, it asks what historians can do with AI discourse as a primary source.

Primary frame · a scholarly gap

Extend Crawford's qualitative claim with quantitative historical evidence: when and how fast the discourse changed.

Secondary frame · a historical parallel

AI rhetoric in the 2010s–20s mirrors nuclear anxiety in the 1950s–60s: fear, national competition, existential threat, calls for government control.

Act I  ·  1990–2013

The Winter and the Rebranding

The story does not begin with growth — it begins with decline. Through the 1990s, “artificial intelligence” steadily falls in books: the long shadow of the AI Winter, when 1980s over-promises collapsed and “AI” carried a stigma of hype. Researchers responded by renaming — “machine learning” rises and overtakes “AI” around 2013. The field rebranded around concrete method.

Act II  ·  2012–2021

Back into the Light

Two forces rescued “AI.” First, the technology started working — deep learning's 2012 ImageNet moment. Second, public spectacle: IBM Watson on Jeopardy! (2011) and, above all, AlphaGo's defeat of Lee Sedol (2016). News coverage climbs from a few thousand to tens of thousands of articles a year — AI moving from the lab bench to the front page.

Act III  ·  2022–2023

The ChatGPT Explosion

ChatGPT (November 2022) put a working AI in everyone's hands and the discourse detonated. But raw counts can mislead — so we measure AI's share of all coverage. Even controlling for the growth of news, AI's slice more than doubled in a single year, to roughly 1% of everything in the press: a genuine shift in collective attention, not a statistical artifact.

The twist

It was never optimism

The conventional story is a journey from scientific optimism to geopolitical anxiety. The tone data complicates it. Measuring the average sentiment of AI coverage shows it was negative in every single year — hovering around −5 — as far back as 2010. There was no golden age of optimism to fall from.

The argument

What changed was scale and salience — not sentiment

Put the pieces together and a sharper claim emerges. AI discourse did not drift from hope to fear; it was wary from the start. What actually changed was scale — the sheer volume of attention exploded after AlphaGo (2016) and ChatGPT (2022–23) — and political salience: AI shifted from a scientific keyword (Deep Blue, research milestones) to a geopolitical one (US–China competition, existential risk, regulation). The anxiety isn't new; its scale and its politics are.

This pattern is not unique to AI. The rhetoric of the 2010s–20s — fear, national competition, existential threat, demands for state control — closely echoes nuclear anxiety of the 1950s–60s. Societies reuse established templates for technological fear. Seen this way, today's AI anxiety becomes historically legible rather than unprecedented: we have run this script before.

Robustness check

Does a second source agree?

What if the 2023 spike were a quirk of Media Cloud? We test it against an independent dataset, GDELT, using its normalized coverage measure (which cancels source growth). Over 2017–2023 the two track each other closely and, in 2023, land at almost the same value (~1.04% vs ~1.05%). Two datasets built by different teams agreeing this precisely means the surge is real, not an artifact.

Sources & method

The methods pair quantitative pattern detection with qualitative historical interpretation — adapting the semantic-shift approach Dr. Woo applied to Cold War newspapers, here turned on AI discourse across three decades.

Conventional sources (framing)

  • Kate Crawford, Atlas of AI (Yale University Press, 2021) — the theoretical frame: AI as a registry of power and extraction.
  • Print archives — Wired, TIME, The Economist (1990–), consulted for how mainstream media framed AI for general audiences.
  • Policy documents — White House AI strategy reports and Congressional records, for how political institutions constructed AI narratives.

Source criticism

  • Google Ngram ends in 2019 and covers books, not journalism — it misses the ChatGPT moment in print entirely.
  • Media Cloud skews toward English-language sources, underrepresenting non-Western perspectives.
  • GDELT's raw counts are unusable — they jump ~10,000× between 2013 and 2015 when GDELT 2.0 expanded its sources. We therefore use only its normalized share and its tone (an average, far less distorted), and flag the small 2010–2013 samples.

These limits are acknowledged as part of the historical argument, not hidden as flaws. Every chart is generated from committed CSVs by a single reproducible script — see the GitHub repository.
